Overview

This tool helps you track your annual tax filing obligations as a full-time employee of a Spanish company under Spain's Beckham Law (Régimen Especial de Trabajadores Desplazados a Territorio Español).

Use the Guide tab to understand each obligation in plain English, and the Checklist tab to track what you've completed each year. Everything auto-saves to your browser's local storage.

What this tool is A personal checklist and reference guide for understanding your filing obligations and deadlines. It helps you stay organized — it does not provide tax advice, calculate amounts, or replace a qualified tax professional (asesor fiscal).

Your Situation at a Glance

  • Residence: Spain (tax resident under Beckham Law)
  • Employment: Full-time employee of a Spanish company
  • Tax regime: IRNR-style taxation (non-resident rules applied to a resident)
  • Income taxed: Spanish-source only (not worldwide) at a flat rate

Beckham Law Basics

How It Works

The Beckham Law (officially: Régimen Especial de Trabajadores Desplazados, Art. 93 LIRPF) allows qualifying workers who move to Spain to be taxed under non-resident income tax rules (IRNR) even though they are tax residents of Spain. In practice, this means:

  • You are a Spanish tax resident for all legal purposes (you live here, have a NIE, are registered in the padrón)
  • But your income tax is calculated under IRNR rules — only Spanish-source income is taxed
  • Employment income from your Spanish employer is taxed at a flat 24% on the first €600,000, and 47% above that
  • You opted in by filing Modelo 149 within 6 months of your Social Security registration

What Is & Isn't Taxed in Spain

Taxed (Spanish-source)

  • Your salary from your Spanish employer — flat 24% (up to €600,000)
  • Spanish rental income (if you own property in Spain)
  • Capital gains from Spanish assets (Spanish stocks, Spanish real estate sales) — taxed at savings rates: 19% on first €6,000, 21% on €6,001–€50,000, 23% on €50,001–€200,000, 27% on €200,001–€300,000, 28% above €300,000
  • Interest from Spanish bank accounts

NOT taxed in Spain (foreign-source)

  • Dividends from non-Spanish stocks (US stocks, international funds, etc.)
  • Interest from non-Spanish bank accounts
  • Capital gains from selling non-Spanish assets
  • Rental income from non-Spanish property
  • Any other income sourced outside Spain
This is the main benefit Your worldwide income outside Spain is simply not taxed by Spain while under Beckham Law. If you have US brokerage accounts, US rental income, or other foreign investment income, Spain does not tax it.

Duration & Renewal

  • Beckham Law applies for the tax year you move to Spain plus the following 5 tax years (6 years total maximum)
  • It is not renewed — once the 6 years expire, you revert to the general IRPF regime (worldwide taxation at progressive rates)
  • You can voluntarily renounce the regime early if it becomes disadvantageous (e.g., if you have very little foreign income)
  • If you leave Spain and return, you generally cannot re-apply (there is a 5-year rule for prior non-residence)
Plan ahead for Year 6+ When Beckham Law expires, Spain will tax your worldwide income. This can significantly impact investment income, US retirement accounts, etc. Start planning 1–2 years before expiry.

Spanish Tax Filings

Modelo 151 — Annual Income Tax Return

This is the key annual filing under Beckham Law. It replaces the standard Modelo 100 (IRPF) for Beckham Law filers.

  • What it is: Your annual Spanish income tax declaration under the special regime
  • Who files: All Beckham Law participants, every year
  • Filing period: Typically April – June 30 of the following year (same window as regular IRPF campaign — Agencia Tributaria announces exact dates each year)
  • What you report: Spanish-source income only (employment, Spanish capital gains, Spanish rental income, etc.)
  • Tax rate applied: Flat 24% on employment income (up to €600k); savings rates on capital gains/interest
  • How to file: Via Agencia Tributaria's Sede Electrónica (online with Cl@ve PIN or digital certificate) or through your asesor fiscal
Your employer withholds tax (retención) Your Spanish employer should be withholding at the 24% flat rate on your salary throughout the year. Modelo 151 is a reconciliation — you declare your total income, subtract withholdings already paid, and either owe the difference or receive a refund.
Do NOT file Modelo 100 Beckham Law filers use Modelo 151, not the standard Modelo 100. Filing the wrong form can create complications. If your asesor mentions "la renta" or "Modelo 100," clarify that you are under the régimen especial.

Modelo 720 — Informative Declaration of Foreign Assets

Modelo 720 requires Spanish tax residents to report foreign assets above certain thresholds. This is an informative declaration (no tax is owed on it), but failure to file carries penalties.

  • Deadline: March 31 of the following year
  • Thresholds (per category): You must report if any category exceeds €50,000:
    • Category 1: Foreign bank accounts
    • Category 2: Foreign securities (stocks, funds, bonds, etc.)
    • Category 3: Foreign real estate
  • Subsequent years: Only re-file if a category increases by more than €20,000 from the last reported value
Beckham Law & Modelo 720: Gray Area Because Beckham Law taxes you under IRNR rules (non-resident), some tax advisors argue Modelo 720 does not apply. However, you ARE legally a Spanish tax resident, and the 720 obligation is tied to tax residency, not the tax regime. The safer approach is to file it. Consult your asesor fiscal for your specific situation.
EU Court ruling (Jan 2022) The CJEU ruled Spain's 720 penalties disproportionate. Since then, Spain reformed the penalty regime (Ley 5/2022). Penalties are now much more reasonable, but the filing obligation itself remains.

Wealth Tax (Impuesto sobre el Patrimonio)

Spain has a wealth tax on net assets. Under Beckham Law:

  • Only Spanish-located assets are subject to wealth tax (not worldwide assets)
  • Filed via Modelo 714 during the regular income tax campaign (April–June)
  • Thresholds and rates vary by Comunidad Autónoma (Madrid has historically had a 100% bonus, effectively eliminating it)
  • The national Impuesto Temporal de Solidaridad de las Grandes Fortunas (solidarity tax) applies if net assets exceed €3 million — this overrides Madrid's regional bonus
For most Beckham Law employees If your Spanish-located assets (Spanish bank accounts, Spanish property, etc.) are under the exempt threshold, wealth tax is unlikely to apply. But check each year.

Social Security

As a full-time employee, your employer handles Social Security contributions:

  • Employee contribution: ~6.35–6.45% of your gross salary (deducted from your paycheck)
  • Employer contribution: ~30–36% (paid by the company, not deducted from you)
  • No action required from you — this is automatic via your nómina
  • Verify contributions on your annual Informe de Vida Laboral (available from Seguridad Social website)

US Tax Filings US PERSON

As a US person (citizen or green card holder), you are required to file US taxes on worldwide income regardless of where you live. This obligation exists in addition to your Spanish obligations.

US taxes you worldwide income Unlike Spain under Beckham Law, the US does not care where your income is sourced. All income — Spanish salary, foreign investments, bank interest, capital gains — must be reported to the IRS.

Form 1040 — US Federal Income Tax Return

  • Standard deadline: April 15
  • Automatic extension for Americans abroad: June 15 (you qualify if your "tax home" is outside the US)
  • Extension available to: October 15 (file Form 4868 by June 15)
  • What you report: All worldwide income — Spanish salary, US/foreign investment income, bank interest, capital gains, everything
  • How to avoid double taxation: Use either the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (Form 2555) or the Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116) — see below
Payment deadline is still April 15 The automatic extension to June 15 extends the filing deadline, not the payment deadline. If you owe tax, you should estimate and pay by April 15 to avoid interest charges. Most Beckham Law filers with proper FTC/FEIE end up owing little or nothing to the IRS.

FBAR — FinCEN Form 114 (Report of Foreign Bank Accounts)

The FBAR is not a tax form — it's a financial reporting requirement administered by FinCEN (Financial Crimes Enforcement Network), not the IRS.

  • Filing threshold: Aggregate value of all foreign financial accounts exceeds $10,000 at any point during the calendar year
  • Deadline: April 15 (automatic extension to October 15, no form needed)
  • What to report: Every non-US financial account you have signatory authority or financial interest over — Spanish bank accounts, Spanish brokerage accounts, any other non-US accounts
  • How to file: Electronically via the BSA E-Filing System
  • Penalties for non-filing: Severe — up to $10,000 per violation (non-willful) or greater of $100,000 or 50% of account balance (willful)
If you have a Spanish bank account, you almost certainly need to file FBAR Even if your Spanish account only holds a few thousand euros, if the aggregate of all your foreign accounts (Spanish bank + any others) exceeded $10,000 at any point in the year, you must file.

FATCA — Form 8938 (Statement of Specified Foreign Financial Assets)

FATCA (Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act) requires reporting of foreign financial assets above certain thresholds. This is filed with your tax return (attached to Form 1040).

  • Thresholds for single filers living abroad:
    • Total value exceeds $200,000 on the last day of the tax year, OR
    • Total value exceeds $300,000 at any time during the tax year
  • What to report: Foreign bank accounts, foreign brokerage accounts, foreign mutual funds, foreign pension plans, ownership in foreign entities, etc.
  • Deadline: Same as your tax return (April 15 / June 15 / October 15 with extension)
  • Relationship to FBAR: Overlapping but separate requirements. Many assets must be reported on both FBAR and Form 8938. File both if you meet both thresholds.

FEIE vs FTC — Avoiding Double Taxation

You must choose one method to avoid paying US tax on income already taxed by Spain:

Option A: Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) — Form 2555

  • Excludes up to ~$126,500 (2024, indexed annually) of foreign earned income from US taxation
  • Must meet either the Bona Fide Residence test or Physical Presence test (330 days abroad in a 12-month period)
  • Only applies to earned income (salary, self-employment) — does NOT cover investment income, capital gains, or interest
  • Cannot be combined with FTC on the same income

Option B: Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) — Form 1116

  • Credits you dollar-for-dollar for foreign taxes paid to Spain
  • Generally more advantageous for Beckham Law filers because the 24% Spanish flat rate on employment income may generate enough foreign tax credits to offset most/all US tax on that income
  • Covers all income types (earned and unearned)
  • Excess credits can be carried forward 10 years or back 1 year
Most common approach for Beckham Law + US Person FTC (Form 1116) is usually preferred. The 24% Spanish tax often exceeds the effective US rate on the same income, generating excess credits. However, investment income sourced outside Spain (which Spain doesn't tax under Beckham) will be fully taxable by the US with no Spanish tax to credit against it.

State Tax Obligations

  • Depends on your last US state of residence before moving abroad
  • Some states (e.g., California, Virginia, New Mexico) may consider you a resident and tax you even while living abroad
  • Other states (e.g., Texas, Florida, Washington, Nevada) have no income tax
  • If your last state was a no-income-tax state: no state filing needed
  • If your last state was a state like California: consult a tax professional about whether you successfully established non-residency

US & Spain Interplay US PERSON

The US–Spain Tax Treaty (Convention for the Avoidance of Double Taxation) provides mechanisms to avoid double taxation. Key points:

Treaty Benefits

  • Employment income: Taxed primarily where performed (Spain), with credits available in the US
  • Dividends: Withholding rates capped at 15% (useful for US dividends received by a Spanish tax resident)
  • Interest: Generally 10% max withholding
  • Capital gains: Generally taxed in the country of residence (Spain for you), except real property gains

Practical Double-Taxation Scenarios

Your Spanish salary

Spain taxes at 24% (Beckham). The US taxes at your marginal rate. Use FTC (Form 1116) to credit the Spanish tax against the US liability. Usually results in zero or minimal US tax on this income.

US-source investment income (dividends, interest, capital gains)

Spain does NOT tax this under Beckham Law. The US DOES tax this. No double taxation here, but you have no foreign tax credit to offset it either — you owe normal US tax on this income.

Spanish bank interest

Spain taxes this (19–28%). The US also taxes it. Use FTC to credit the Spanish tax.

Key Insight for Beckham + US Person Beckham Law's benefit (Spain doesn't tax foreign income) means the US side gets no foreign tax credit on that income. For income that Spain doesn't tax (US dividends, foreign capital gains), you pay normal US tax. Net effect: Beckham saves you Spanish tax but you still owe US tax on worldwide non-Spanish income.

Filing Order

In practice, most filers:

  1. File FBAR by April 15 (or let it auto-extend to October 15)
  2. File Modelo 720 by March 31 (if applicable)
  3. Extend US return to October 15 (because you need your Spanish tax info first)
  4. File Modelo 151 by June 30
  5. File US return (Form 1040 + Form 1116/2555 + Form 8938) once you know your final Spanish tax liability
Why extend the US return? You need to know exactly how much Spanish tax you paid (from Modelo 151) to accurately calculate your Foreign Tax Credit on Form 1116. Since Spain's filing deadline is June 30, extending the US return to October 15 gives you that information.

Common Gotchas

Spanish Side

  • Filing the wrong form: Use Modelo 151, NOT Modelo 100. This is the most common mistake.
  • Employer withholding rate: Your employer should withhold at 24%, not the progressive IRPF rates. Verify on your nómina.
  • Modelo 720 confusion: Even though Beckham taxes you like a non-resident, you ARE a tax resident. Most advisors recommend filing 720 if you exceed the thresholds.
  • Forgetting Modelo 149: If you didn't file this when you first arrived, you may not be validly under Beckham Law. Check with your asesor.
  • Capital gains classification: Gains from Spanish assets use savings rates (19–28%), not the flat 24%. These are separate categories on Modelo 151.
  • Stock options / RSUs: Complex under Beckham Law. The taxable event timing and sourcing rules differ. Get professional advice if you have equity compensation.
  • Beckham Law expiry: When your 6 years end, you move to the regular regime. Foreign income becomes taxable. Plan ahead.

US Side US PERSON

  • FBAR penalties are disproportionate: The $10,000 threshold is low. If you have a Spanish checking account and a Spanish brokerage account, you probably exceed it. File every year.
  • PFIC trap: Non-US mutual funds (including many Spanish/European funds) are treated as PFICs (Passive Foreign Investment Companies) by the IRS, resulting in punitive taxation. Stick to US-domiciled ETFs/funds where possible.
  • Spanish pension plan contributions: May not be deductible on your US return. Employer pension contributions may be treated as taxable income by the IRS.
  • Currency conversion: All amounts on US forms must be in USD. Use the IRS yearly average exchange rate or the spot rate on the transaction date (be consistent).
  • FEIE + FTC interaction: You cannot use both on the same income. If using FEIE, any excess Spanish tax on excluded income cannot generate FTC.
  • Estimated tax payments: If you expect to owe US tax (e.g., on investment income not taxed by Spain), consider quarterly estimated payments to avoid penalties.
